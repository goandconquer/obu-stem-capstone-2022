Welcome to STEM Day at Oklahoma Baptist University!

What is STEM Day?

STEM is an acronym that stands for (S)cience, (T)echnology, (E)ngineering, and (M)athematics. The acronym usually accompanies any sorts of programs or activities that emphasize learning in these fields and typically is targeted towards the younger generations. Throughout the world, the world's biggest problems are solved using these core educational subjects, so it's important that students across the world are provided the opportunity to engage with the tools and techniques that enable us to create amazing things that we use everyday!

Why OBU?

As a Christian liberal arts university, OBU is dedicated to the pursuit of raising up the next generation with not only the knowledge and experience young people need to begin their career, but also the virtues and ethics necessary to bring out positive change in the world, both today and into the future.

What programs are being showcased?

Encrpytion Fundamentals

Technical computing has its origin in the obfuscation of vital information. People who needed to hide critical details about their efforts (often in times of conflict) from hostile parties would rely on complex algorithm-like encodings that would mask their secrets behind difficult-to-crack ciphertexts. This site showcases three different types of encoding methods (Caeser Cipher, Rail Fence Cipher, and Substitution Cipher), as well as how they work and how they are decrypted. Our STEM Day demonstration allows participants to interactively use data analyzing techniques that are often used to understand ciphertext generated through the Substitution Cipher.

Data Classification

Across the world, statistical information is collected over hundreds of thousands of different topics. That data, to be useful, has to be processed in a feasiblely efficient and engaging way. Understanding the mathematical foundations behind interpretting this data is an unprecedented skill in the modern day. Our STEM Day demonstration allowed participants to interactively engage with data-collection methods, and understand how that data is then classified into a meaningful numerical entry.
